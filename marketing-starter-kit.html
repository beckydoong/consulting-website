Prompt 01 · Customer Research

1.The Audience Voice Decoder

The problem

Most marketing copy fails for one reason: it doesn't sound like the customer. Founders write copy that sounds like the founder. AI writes copy that sounds like AI. Both lose.

The fastest way to fix this isn't a copywriting course — it's mining the language your customers already use. Their words live in your support tickets, product reviews, social DMs, sales call transcripts, and survey responses. You just need a way to extract the patterns.

Why it works

Voice-of-customer copy converts 2-5x better than founder-voiced copy on the same offer. When prospects see their own pain in your headline, they don't have to translate. They feel understood — which is the precondition for trust.

The prompt

Audience Voice Decoder You are a customer research analyst. I will paste below a collection of [reviews / support tickets / DMs / survey responses] from my customers.

Analyze them and extract:

1. The top 5 pain points in the customer's own words — quote them verbatim where possible.
2. The top 3 transformation outcomes they describe (what life looks like AFTER the problem is solved).
3. The exact phrases and metaphors they repeat (these become your headlines).
4. The objections they raise about products like mine — and the reasons they finally said yes.
5. The competitors or alternatives they mention by name.

For each insight, give me the verbatim quote that supports it. Don't paraphrase.

My product/service: [describe in 2 sentences]
Here's the data:
—
[paste 20-100 pieces of customer text]
Pain point: "I'm spending 14 hours a week writing emails and most of them get ignored."
Transformation: "I want to wake up to qualified leads in my inbox, not have to chase every one of them."
Repeated phrase: "marketing engine" (used by 7 of 23 customers)
Objection → resolution: "I thought AI would make my emails sound robotic. What sold me was seeing a sample that sounded like me."

Pro tips

  • Don't summarize first. Paste raw data. AI's value is finding patterns YOU missed.
  • Aim for 50+ data points. Anything less and the patterns are noise.
  • Run it quarterly. Customer language drifts as your audience evolves.
  • Use the verbatim quotes as headline tests. Don't rewrite them.
Prompt 02 · Email Marketing

2.The Revenue Email Machine

The problem

Email is still the highest-ROI channel for almost every business. A solid lifecycle program — welcome, nurture, sales, win-back — can do six figures of revenue without a single new lead. But most lean teams either don't write email sequences at all or write generic ones that get ignored.

The reason: writing emails takes hours, and most founders don't have a system. So they batch one campaign at a time, in a panic, before a launch.

Why it works

This prompt loads your voice, audience, and past campaign data into one persistent context. After that initial setup, you generate full sequences in 20 minutes instead of 20 hours. The first draft is 80% there. You edit, you send.

One of my clients went from 14 hours/week on customer emails to 2 hours/week using this prompt as a foundation. Same conversion rates. 12 hours back.

The prompt

Revenue Email Machine You are my email copywriter. You write in MY voice and for MY audience. Here's the context — keep it loaded:

My business: [1 sentence what you sell + who for]
Voice: [3 adjectives + 1 thing I never say]
Audience pain points: [paste output from Prompt #1]
Last 3 high-performing emails I sent: [paste subject lines + opening 100 words of each]
Last 3 emails that flopped: [same]

Now write me a [welcome / nurture / sales / win-back] sequence with the following structure:

Email 1: [goal — e.g., deliver the lead magnet + set expectations]
Email 2: [goal]
Email 3: [goal]
Email 4: [goal]
Email 5: [goal]

For each email give me: subject line (under 50 chars), preview text (under 100 chars), full body (250-400 words), and a single clear CTA. Match my voice exactly — don't add corporate fluff or buzzwords.
Subject: How a $7M client doubled in 12 months (32 chars, curiosity + proof)
Preview: A small in-house team, no expansion, no new tools. Here's what we changed.
Body opens: "Hi [first name], Want to share a story I think will encourage you. One of my clients came to me sitting at $7M..."

Pro tips

  • Build the context once. Save your voice + audience block as a Claude project, GPT custom instructions, or a snippet you paste at the top of every chat.
  • Always feed it your historical opens/clicks data. AI generates dramatically better subject lines when it can pattern-match your past wins.
  • Edit the first email by hand. Then ask the AI to "rewrite emails 2-5 to match the voice and rhythm of my edit." This is the biggest unlock.
Prompt 03 · Content Strategy

3.The Content Repurposer

The problem

You spend three hours writing a great blog post or recording a 40-minute podcast. Then it lives on one channel and dies. Meanwhile your competitors who post less insightful content are everywhere, because they post it everywhere.

The bottleneck isn't ideas — it's translation. Turning one pillar piece into platform-native posts (LinkedIn, X, Instagram, email, threads, shorts scripts) used to take half a day. Now it takes 20 minutes.

Why it works

Each platform rewards different formats: LinkedIn wants long-form storytelling, X wants compressed hooks, Instagram wants visual narrative, email wants 1:1 voice. This prompt does the translation in parallel, preserving your core idea while reshaping for each medium.

One pillar piece = 10 derivative posts = three weeks of content from one afternoon of work.

The prompt

Content Repurposer You are a multi-channel content strategist. I'm giving you ONE pillar piece of content. Turn it into 10 platform-native posts.

Pillar piece: [paste full blog post, podcast transcript, video script, OR detailed outline of a talk]
My audience: [founders + lean teams growing X / SaaS marketers / etc.]
My voice: [3 adjectives]

Produce ALL of these, each clearly labeled:

1. LinkedIn post (150-200 words, hook in line 1, story structure, no hashtags spam, single CTA)
2. LinkedIn carousel outline (10 slides, headline + 2-sentence body each)
3. X thread (7-10 tweets, hook tweet under 240 chars, payoff at the end)
4. Email newsletter (350 words, conversational tone, one CTA at the end)
5. Instagram caption + hook (under 150 words, line breaks, 1 question to drive comments)
6. YouTube Short / TikTok script (45-60 seconds, hook in first 3 seconds)
7. Lead magnet idea (1-paragraph pitch for a free PDF or template based on this content)
8. Sales objection rebuttal (how this content addresses a real buyer hesitation)
9. 3 quote graphics (each quote ≤ 20 words, designed to stand alone)
10. 1 follow-up post for next week that builds on the pillar without repeating it

Don't soften the original insight. Each format should feel native to its platform, not like a recycled snippet.
Pillar piece: "Why most lean teams fail at email marketing"
LinkedIn post output: "I worked with a founder who was sending 4 emails a month and getting nowhere. We ran an audit. The emails weren't bad. The problem was she didn't have a system. Here's the 3-step fix that took her from 8% open rate to 41%..."
X thread output: "Lean teams don't fail at email because they can't write. They fail because they don't have a system. Here's the one I give every client. 🧵"

Pro tips

  • Start from the BEST pillar you have. Repurposing weak content makes 10 weak posts.
  • Schedule the outputs over 3 weeks, not all in one day. Algorithms reward consistency.
  • Hand to a VA or junior teammate. The AI does the translation; a human does the publishing.
Prompt 04 · Email Optimization

4.The Subject Line Lab

The problem

The single biggest determinant of whether your email gets read isn't the body — it's the subject line. A 5% lift on open rate compounds into thousands of dollars over the year. But most teams write one subject line, send the email, and never test alternatives.

The reason isn't laziness. It's that subject line writing feels like guesswork. You don't know what'll work until it's too late to change.

Why it works

This prompt generates 20 subject line variants based on your historical data, then scores each one by predicted open rate using the patterns from your past wins. You pick the top 3, A/B test, and learn what works for YOUR audience.

Done quarterly, this alone can lift your email revenue 20-40%.

The prompt

Subject Line Lab You are an email subject line strategist. I'm sending an email with the following details:

Email body summary: [2-3 sentence summary]
Email goal: [drive clicks to X / get replies / book calls / sell Y]
Audience: [who they are + what they care about]

My historical top-10 subject lines by open rate:
[paste 10 subject lines + their open rate %]

My historical bottom-10 subject lines by open rate:
[same — what FAILED]

Generate 20 subject line variants. For each one, include:
— Character count (target under 50 for mobile)
— Predicted open rate range (low / mid / high based on patterns from my historical data)
— Why it'll work (1 sentence — what pattern it matches from my winners)
— One variant that's a "risk" — counter-intuitive but with high upside if it lands

Rank them in a table from highest predicted open rate to lowest. Don't include emoji unless my historical winners use them.
| Subject | Chars | Predicted | Pattern match |
| "Why are you here, Becky?" | 24 | HIGH | Matches your top winner's personalization pattern |
| "The prompt that gave one team 12 hours back" | 45 | HIGH | Story + specific number — your audience clicks these |
| "I owe you an apology" | 22 | RISK | Curiosity gap; could underperform if seen as clickbait |

Pro tips

  • Always test the top 2. Use your email platform's A/B test feature with 20% of the list.
  • Keep a running spreadsheet of subject + open rate. The AI gets exponentially better with more data.
  • Watch for fatigue. A formula that wins for 6 months can suddenly stop working. Refresh quarterly.
Prompt 05 · Conversion Optimization

5.The Sales Page Diagnostic

The problem

You're driving traffic to a landing page. You can see the visitor count. But you can't see WHY most of them leave without converting. A CRO consultant charges $3-5K for one audit. Most founders never get one.

The fix isn't redesigning the page from scratch. It's identifying the 3-5 highest-leverage fixes — the friction points, the missing proof, the weak hooks — and addressing those first.

Why it works

This prompt walks through your page using the same checklist a senior conversion strategist would: scan-ability, value clarity, social proof placement, objection handling, CTA hierarchy. It outputs a prioritized fix list with effort/impact estimates so you know what to do this week vs. this quarter.

The prompt

Sales Page Diagnostic You are a conversion rate optimization specialist with 15 years of experience auditing landing pages. I'm pasting my page below. Audit it as if I'd paid you $3,000.

Page URL: [url]
What I'm selling: [product/service + price]
Target visitor: [who, where they came from, what stage]
Current conversion rate: [X%, or "I don't know"]
Goal CR: [Y%]

Page copy and structure:
[paste full page text in order, or paste a screenshot description]

Audit against these 8 dimensions and give me a score 1-10 for each, with specific evidence:

1. Headline clarity — does it pass the "5-second test"?
2. Hook strength — does the first 100 words make me want to keep reading?
3. Value clarity — do I know what I'm getting and what changes for me?
4. Social proof — testimonials, case studies, logos, results — placed where they matter?
5. Objection handling — what doubts does the page leave unanswered?
6. CTA hierarchy — is the primary action obvious and frictionless?
7. Trust signals — credentials, guarantees, refund policy, security?
8. Scan-ability — does someone skimming get the core message?

Then give me a PRIORITIZED FIX LIST with effort (S/M/L) and predicted impact (low/medium/high) for each fix. Start with the highest-leverage 5 changes I could ship this week.
Headline clarity: 6/10 — "Marketing Systems for Founders" is clear but generic. Add specificity: "...that pay for themselves in 90 days."

Top 3 fixes this week:
1. Add a single hero proof point (S effort, HIGH impact) — visitors leave because there's no signal of who else trusted you.
2. Move the FAQ above the final CTA (S effort, MEDIUM impact) — your page raises questions you don't answer until they've already decided.
3. Shorten the bio paragraph from 4 paragraphs to 1 + a "more about me" link (S effort, MEDIUM impact).

Pro tips

  • Run this on your top 3 pages — homepage, top lead magnet page, top sales page.
  • Re-audit after every major copy change. Small wins compound.
  • Take the "risk" recommendations seriously. The biggest CR lifts usually come from changes that feel counter-intuitive.
Prompt 06 · Competitive Intelligence

6.The Competitor Teardown

The problem

You vaguely know who your competitors are. You've probably visited their website. But you don't have a clear picture of their funnel — what lead magnets they offer, what their email sequences do, what their pricing strategy is, where they're winning and where they're weak.

Without that view, you're guessing at positioning. With it, you can find gaps you can exploit in days, not months.

Why it works

This prompt is a structured reverse-engineering exercise. You feed it publicly available info — their homepage, pricing page, opt-in flow, social presence — and it returns a teardown organized by funnel stage, with specific gaps and opportunities you can use.

Run it on your top 3 competitors. You'll come out with positioning angles your team can ship in a week.

The prompt

Competitor Teardown You are a competitive strategy analyst. I want a teardown of a competitor's marketing funnel based on what I've gathered publicly.

Competitor: [name + URL]
What I do: [your business + ideal customer]
Where we overlap: [product category, audience, etc.]

What I've collected on the competitor:
— Homepage hero copy: [paste]
— Pricing or main offer: [paste]
— Lead magnet name + what they do with the lead: [describe]
— Email sequence I got from them (subjects + first lines if possible): [paste]
— Their primary social channel + last 5 posts: [summarize]
— Visible customer testimonials / case study patterns: [describe]

Now give me:

1. Their positioning in 1 sentence — what specific buyer + problem do they own?
2. Their funnel architecture — top-of-funnel through paid offer, walked step by step.
3. Their 3 strongest moves — what's working that I should be aware of.
4. Their 3 biggest weaknesses — gaps in the offer, messaging, or experience.
5. 3 specific moves I can make this month to win prospects who are evaluating us both.
Positioning: "They own the 'agency for nonprofits over $5M' niche."

Weaknesses:
1. Their welcome sequence is generic — same copy regardless of which lead magnet you grabbed.
2. No founder voice. Feels corporate. Buyers buying lean marketing want a human.
3. Pricing isn't published — adds friction for self-serve evaluators.

Counter-moves:
1. Personalize your welcome sequences based on lead source (which we do for ours).
2. Lead with the founder voice in all comms.
3. Publish pricing transparently with a "what you get" breakdown.

Pro tips

  • Subscribe to your competitors' email lists. One day of data → months of strategy.
  • Don't copy. Differentiate. The point is to find what they CAN'T or WON'T do well.
  • Refresh annually. Funnels evolve. Last year's gaps may be filled.
Prompt 07 · Brand Narrative

7.The Founder Story Polisher

The problem

If you're a founder-led business, your story IS your marketing. The "why" behind the company is what makes prospects remember you, refer you, and trust you with money — especially in a saturated market where every competitor sounds the same.

But most founders tell their story badly. Either too modestly ("oh, I just kind of fell into this") or too pitch-y ("our world-class team has 30+ years..."). The version that converts is structured, specific, and emotionally honest.

Why it works

This prompt takes the raw materials of your origin story and shapes it into the narrative form that consistently drives trust: a specific moment, a turning point, a hard-won insight, and an offer to help others avoid the same pain.

Use the output on your About page, in your podcast intros, at the top of cold pitches, and inside your welcome sequence Email #2.

The prompt

Founder Story Polisher You are a brand strategist who specializes in founder-led businesses. I want to turn my origin story into a magnetic brand narrative I can use across my website, sales pitches, and content.

Answer these for me, then I'll give you my raw material:

1. The "before" moment — what were you doing or feeling before you started this business? Be specific (not "I was unhappy" but "I was sending my 4th cold pitch that morning at 6am while my coffee got cold").
2. The trigger — what specific event, conversation, or realization made you act?
3. The hard period — what nearly killed it in the first year?
4. The insight — what did you learn that contrarians wouldn't have figured out?
5. The "now" — what are you doing today that the "before" you wouldn't have believed?
6. The "you" promise — what specific outcome do you help OTHER people get because of what you learned?

My raw answers:
[paste your answers — or paste a transcript of you telling the story to a friend]

Now give me 3 versions of my story:
— 150-word version (for About page hero, podcast bios)
— 400-word version (for About page full, Email #2 of welcome sequence)
— 50-word elevator version (for cold outreach, LinkedIn DMs, intros at events)

Use specific sensory detail. Avoid clichés like "I'm passionate about..." and "I've always loved..." Lead with the moment, not the credentials.
50-word version: "I spent 15 years running digital teams that raised $300M for organizations with millions of subscribers. Then I started seeing founders burn out trying to do the same with three people. So I built Seedscale — to give lean teams the systems that used to need a 10-person department."

Pro tips

  • The best stories include a specific number. "$300M raised" beats "tons of revenue."
  • Skip the credentials until paragraph 3. Lead with the human moment.
  • Re-record your story to a friend and transcribe it. The way you tell it in conversation is usually better than what you write at a keyboard.
  • Iterate every 6 months. As your business grows, the story changes.
